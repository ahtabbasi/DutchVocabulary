## Tips for Regular Verb Conjugation

- **Present tense:** Remove -en from the infinitive, add appropriate endings (-, -t, -en)
- **Perfectum:** Use "hebben" or "zijn" + past participle (ge- + verb stem + -d/-t)
- **Imperfectum:** Remove -en, add -de/-te endings based on the verb stem

## Notes on Sentence Structures

**Inversie (Inversion):** Occurs when the subject and verb switch places. This happens in:
- Yes/no questions (sentences 2, 5, 9, 13, 17)
- Question word questions (sentences 2, 9, 13, 15)
- Sentences starting with time/place expressions or subordinate clauses (sentences 4, 7, 10, 16, 19)

**Main Clause + Subordinate Clause:** When a main clause is followed by a subordinate clause introduced by "dat" (that), "omdat" (because), "als" (if), "waar" (where), etc. (sentences 6, 12, 13, 15, 18)

**Subordinate Clause + Main Clause:** When a subordinate clause comes first, followed by a comma and then the main clause. This causes inversie in the main clause. (sentences 4, 7, 10, 16, 19)

**Modal Verbs:** Dutch modal verbs include kunnen (can), moeten (must/should), willen (want), mogen (may), and zullen (will/shall). When used with another verb, the second verb stays in infinitive form. (sentences 5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 20)

**Reflexive Verbs:** These verbs use reflexive pronouns (zich, me, je, zich, zich, zich, zich) that refer back to the subject. Common reflexive verbs include: zich wassen (to wash oneself), zich aankleden (to dress oneself), zich herinneren (to remember), zich voorstellen (to introduce oneself), zich gedragen (to behave oneself), etc. (sentences 1, 2, 3, 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 18, 19, 20)
